Assignment

General context: The habitability of the Earth is threatened for all forms of life by the alteration of the complex interactions between living organisms and the geo-ecosystem (air, water, rocks and soils) to which they are connected. As also highlighted in the 2019 Summary for Policymakers of the IPBES Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services: “sustainability can only be achieved by current trajectories, and the goals for 2030 and beyond can only be achieved by transformative changes across economic, social, political and technological factors.”

Interdisciplinarity: a necessity! The ecological emergency therefore calls on scientists to make a substantial effort towards interdisciplinarity between Earth, environmental, technological and digital sciences, in order to develop relevant innovative solutions and techniques to document, in the long term, the evolution of natural and anthropized systems. In this context, the permanent observation systems of the natural environment have a fundamental role, but these must be "augmented" so that they are able to provide the relevant data and services to implement systemic approaches to relevant spatial and temporal scales, while integrating the challenge of their energy sobriety.

Context of environmental observations: It is necessary to rethink observation strategies to redefine the meaning and scope of data acquired in situ. This includes rethinking the communication and data processing infrastructure that accompanies heterogeneous sensor networks, replacing a centralized (cloud) system where all data are recorded, to evolve towards a (fog/edge) decentralized system where intelligence is located as close as possible to the sensors, in order to meet the needs for agility and energy and digital frugality. The new observation strategy must therefore take into account thematic challenges (intermittency of the processes of interest), material (diversity of data flows generated, presence of fixed and mobile sensors) and digital (variable processing load, heterogeneity of data networks, LoRa access, Wi-Fi, 4/5G).

Objective of the thesis: The objective of this thesis is to design and adapt the Fog-computing system developed by IRISA to meet the specific needs of environmental observation. The proposed solutions will be tested and qualified in-natura on the “green cross,” an area of the Beaulieu campus which has been renatured. This close common space, at the interface between laboratory and research observatory, aims to promote a vision of interdisciplinarity through experimentation, and offers a context conducive to the involvement of students during practical work. It will strengthen interactions between the units of 2 major research labs of the university (environmental systems, science and information technology).

Main activities

Methodological approach: The scientific challenge is to meet the specific needs of a fog computing type platform capable of meeting the specific needs of researchers in environmental sciences:

  • Allow the fast and efficient processing of collected data to update the observation strategy (increase in sampling frequencies, control of a sampling action) or run a numerical model that describes the data and thus define a reaction to a unforeseen event.. The same platform must be able to support several simultaneous applications with a great variability of volumes of data to process, intensity of calculation, etc.

  • Enable fully autonomous execution without human intervention. Observation platforms may eventually be deployed in inhospitable environments, where in particular the energy supply will be based solely on renewable sources (e.g. solar). This creates difficult system issues in adapting treatments to varying amounts of available energy. In periods of low sunlight, it may for example become necessary to turn off part of the platform to keep only the highest priority treatments.

We will use an experimental research methodology based on the deployment of a fog computing platform in the “Green Cross” sector of the Beaulieu campus. This prototype will be used both to define the scientific issues of the thesis, as an environment for experimental validation of the proposed solutions, and as a life-size environmental observation platform.

 

Main stages of the thesis:

  • Step 1: the first step will consist of a study of the uses of existing platforms, their qualities and their limitations. This bibliographic study may give rise to a first scientific publication.
  • Step 2: the second step will allow the definition of a basic software platform which will serve as a starting point for the thesis. This platform based on existing tools will have to remain as simple as possible while allowing a certain flexibility in the uses. It will be deployed in real conditions (energy autonomy) in the green cross sector of the Beaulieu campus. This work may give rise to a second scientific publication.
  • Step 3: the rest of the thesis will be done incrementally through the analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of the initial platform, and the proposal of new techniques to improve its operation and energy autonomy.
  • Step 4: the last step will focus on writing the thesis manuscript and preparing the defense.
