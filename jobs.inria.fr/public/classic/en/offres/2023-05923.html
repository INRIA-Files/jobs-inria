Context

Acumes project-team is a joint team between Inria and mathematics laboratory Jean-Alexandre Dieudonné (LJAD) at Université Côte d'Azur. The research carried out focuses on analysis and optimisation of systems governed by partial differential equations, with multidisciplinary applications ranging from fluid and structural mechanics to the modelling of biological phenomena, road and pedestrian traffic. The team is now interested in deep learning methods to efficiently combine data and physical models.

Assignment

Optimal design is a key ingredient to achieve a sustainable growth in the forthcoming years in several domains (e.g. buildings, aeronautics, ground transportation, etc.), accounting for several criteria such as energy consumption, pollutant emissions, acoustic comfort or materials life cycle management. Current approaches rely on standard simulation tools (e.g., finite-element solvers) coupled to optimization toolboxes, which require a deep expertise of related coupled disciplines (fluid and structure mechanics, heat transfer, acoustics, etc.) and yield low efficiency procedures due to the presence of several embedded iterative loops.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) paradigms have led to disruptive approaches in many fields, especially those with massive data and for which there is no established model. However, the direct use of AI techniques is not straightforward here, because experimental data do not exist  at early design phases while simulation data are too computationally expensive to generate sufficiently large training datasets.

An alternate methodology emerged in 2019, namely Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs), which targets to predict physical solutions using multi-layer perceptrons trained to satisfy physical laws at a set of space/time sampling points. Contrary to classical AI paradigms, no data is thus required for the learning process. The key step is the minimization of a loss function gathering the residuals of all physical models, boundary conditions and coupling conditions. 

This project aims at proposing a new AI framework for multidisciplinary design based on physics-informed neural networks. This task involves both methodological and applicative issues.

From the methodological point of view, it has been shown that the minimization of the loss function, defined as a weighted sum of several criteria, yields poor results using classical algorithms (e.g. Adams or L-BFGS) due to the conflicting nature of these criteria and the ill-conditioning of the resulting problem. Therefore, we propose to investigate the use of more sophisticated optimization methods, inspired from multi-objective or constrained optimization to mitigate efficiently concurrent criteria. Note that improvements in this area may also benefit other general AI issues.

From an applicative viewpoint, design optimization of multidisciplinary systems is a challenging problem involving several physical phenomena (fluid and structure mechanics, heat transfer, acoustics) coupled altogether, associated to shape / topology / parameter optimization in large dimension spaces including constraints and antagonistic criteria. An objective of this work is therefore to investigate the capability of PINNs to solve these problems in a versatile and efficient way, beyond existing approaches. In particular, we target to solve the design problem and the modeling problem simultaneously, in a one-shot way, to significantly reduce the computational costs related to multidisciplinary design optimization. As a result, we also aim at providing software demonstrators to the community, to foster new collaborations and boost the development of physics-based AI paradigms in this field.

He/she will present his/her work at international conferences on the subject and write articles for specialized journals.
