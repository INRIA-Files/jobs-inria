Context

Micro-organisms are key players in almost all ecosystems, from the human gut to the ocean.
Additionally, they represent an invaluable resource for a variety of markets (health, green
chemistry, agro-industry...). Understanding and predicting their growth is thus of great
importance, but it is also a big challenge given the variable environment in which they thrive and
their capacity to acclimate (i.e., to change their biochemical compositions in response to
environment variation).
Given the recent developments in sequencing, metabolic networks of various micro-organisms
are now available. These networks can be used to predict microbial growth. Actually, assuming
that microorganisms have maximized their growth rate through evolution, one can determine the
optimal fluxes in the metabolic network for balanced growth conditions. This approach, called
Flux Balance Analysis (FBA), has allowed to better understand microbial growth (Orth et al.,
Nature biotech, 2010). Nonetheless, it is restricted to balanced growth conditions, with
additionally a fixed biochemical composition. On the other hand, microbial resource allocation problems allow to deal with variable composition (Giordano et al., Plos Comp Bio, 2016; Reimers
et al., PNAS, 2017). Nonetheless, coupling these approaches is not straightforward. As a
consequence, no method allows nowadays to predict from its genome the growth rate and
composition of a microorganism under a variable environment. In this context, the objective of
this thesis is to develop and validate a methodology based on optimality that allows to predict
from the metabolic network of a microorganism its growth rate, proteome and biochemical
composition under dynamic environmental conditions.

Assignment

Thesis work:
The first objective will be to develop mathematical microbial growth models based on ordinary
differential equations. Different levels of realism and complexity will be considered depending on
the details included in the metabolic network. The initial detailed network will be reduced using
quasi steady-states approximation (as proposed in Baroukh et al. (Plos One, 2014)). Proteome
resource allocation will be formalized as an optimization problem, assuming as objective the
maximization of growth rate. The proposed problems will then be studied both analytically (using
the Pontryagin Maximum Principle and Optimal Control theory) and numerically to determine
optimal allocation strategy.
Then, a dedicated method for calibrating the model will be developed. Actually, predictive
methods based on microbial resource allocation involve a number of (unknown) parameters,
ranging from a few for coarse-grained models to hundreds, even thousands for genome-scale
models. Model calibration with experimental data is thus a necessary (but for now omitted) step
to go from qualitative results to quantitative predictions. To this end, a strategy for parameter
identification will be setup by considering the solution of a bi-level optimization problem. The
resulting complex optimization problem will be solved inspired by recent developments (Sinha et
al., 2017).
The method will be developed with two microorganisms, the bacterium Escherichia coli (using
experimental data from the literature) and the microalga Tisochrysis lutea (using experiments
carried out at the Ifremer Physiology and Biotechnology of Algae Laboratory).
