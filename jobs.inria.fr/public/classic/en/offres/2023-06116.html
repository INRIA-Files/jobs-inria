Assignment

The PhD student will aim at developing models of cancer cell populations in order to better understand and predict their fate and then develop drug control strategies to limit their growth or even obtain their eradication. The models built will be based on ordinary and delay differential equations, discretely structuring the population according to the aggressiveness of the cells. The thesis will be carried out in three steps: first, a modeling work will be carried out via the simplification or reduction of existing models; then nonlinear control methods aiming at globally stabilizing an equilibrium without cancer cells will be developed; and finally theoretical and computational optimal control approaches will be considered in order to optimize the efficiency of the treatment while limiting its harmful effects.

Background:
Among the multiple disorders that can affect living organisms, cancer is of paramount importance for various reasons: its severity and aggressiveness (among the first causes of death), its extension to all tissues, its distribution to all age groups, the high complexity of the mechanisms that trigger it (e.g. genetic or epigenetic mutations), the difficulty to predict its evolution and the multiplicity of treatment protocols. The modeling of cancer cell populations can help us to improve our understanding of the mechanisms of cancer initiation, to interpret certain behaviors during the evolution of the disease, as well as to better understand certain phenomena of resistance to therapies, with the aim of proposing more and more adapted therapeutic schemes.

Thesis objectives:
The first objective will be to develop models of growth and competition between distinct cell populations, based on the models proposed in [1,2,3]. These models will be used to describe the dynamics of healthy and cancerous cell populations. In particular, the co-existence of ordinary stem cells and cells with a progressively increasing capacity for proliferation - which is a characteristic of cancer cells - will be the focus of this work. From a mathematical point of view, these models will be mainly described by ordinary differential equations, possibly with time delays, and including some time-varying parameters. A particularity of the models will be that they will be more realistic and will take into account recent observations that associate cancer with a precise succession of genetic and epigenetic mutations ([6]). Biological functionalities of healthy and diseased cells, such as differentiation, apoptosis and mitosis, can be altered by the secretion of growth factors or by the injection of anti-cancer treatments. Therefore, some variables in the model may be directly controlled by drug intake (as in [10]). In particular, the models will include therapeutic control variables with two different mechanisms (as in [2]): chemotherapy (which induces mortality), and targeted therapy (which reduces cell proliferation). The new models thus developed will have rich dynamics, capable of reproducing behaviors observed in cancer cell populations such as plasticity, cell cycle block, dormancy of mutated cells, etc.

The second challenge will be to develop state-space methods, based for example on Lyapunov functions ([5]), for the analysis of the stability of equilibrium points, as well as for the stabilization of the trajectories of the models considered around a desired operating point. Thus, the modeling and analysis methods aim at a better understanding of some biological mechanisms related to cancer and its therapy.

The last objective will be to apply optimal control theory and Pontryagin's maximum principle ([6,7]), similar to [8,9], in order to determine new therapeutic schemes likely to improve anti-cancer treatments. These strategies would seek to eradicate cancer cells or to maintain them in a dormant state, while minimizing for example the risks related to the toxicity of the treatments (several therapeutic performance criteria will be tested).

Method :
The main concepts addressed in the thesis: the use of modeling techniques for healthy and diseased cell populations, stability/stabilization and robustness analysis in dynamical systems, the study of different classes of controlled dynamical systems (possibly time-varying or switching). Development of optimal control strategies. The application of Pontraygin's principle. The development of direct and indirect optimization methods to characterize optimal or sub-optimal control strategies, etc.
Software: Matlab, Python/Julia, BOCOP (optimal control solver)

Collaborations:
This thesis topic follows from existing collaborations with Jean Clairambault (Inria Paris - modeling, cancer) and Catherine Bonnet (L2S Paris-Saclay - automation, stability analysis, delay systems). They will therefore be involved in the points of the thesis where their expertise is relevant. The thesis will also benefit from a collaboration with Hitay Özbay, Bilkent University (Ankara, Turkey) on the analysis of population dynamics, cancer control and modeling.
