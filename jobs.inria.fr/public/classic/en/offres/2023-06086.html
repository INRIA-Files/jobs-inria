Main activities

PhD topic.

Inverse magnetisation problem consists in inferring information about a static magnetic source from partial knowledge of the produced field. The PhD thesis will be concerned with several mathematical challenges arising from such a problem in the paleomagnetic context where the ultimate goal is to get insight into evolution of magnetic fields of Earth or other celestial bodies. Some georocks contain valuable remanent magnetisation that can be extracted by analysis of ultra-weak magnetic fields measured with scanning microscopy devices, e.g. using SQUID (Superconducting Quantum Interference Device) or QDM (Quantum Diamond Microscope), on a patch of the plane passing at some distance from a sliced sample. While the general inverse magnetisation problem is severely ill-posed [1], relevant information such as the net magnetisation vector of a sample can be uniquely recovered and is still of great value for geophysicists. Moreover, under additional assumptions on the sample, the whole magnetisation distribution could also be estimated.
Motivated by particular setups at EAPS (Earth, Atmospheric & Planetary Sciences) Department, MIT (Massachusetts Institute of Technology, USA) and Harvard Paleomagnetics Lab (USA), the aim of the thesis work will be advancing on the aspects related to the limitation of the available measured data, high noise content of the measurements and inapplicability of the current methodology to deal with more general sources and particularities of the experimental setting.
To be more precise, most of the current methodology is able to deal with samples whose magnetisation is localised compared to the measurement area.
Field extension techniques, under active development at present, are not yet satisfactory for two reasons. First, they apply only to planar samples and regular magnetisation distributions. Second, obtaining results of a good quality strongly depends on some computationally heavy procedures. This has to be improved to make dipolar/multipolar approximations [2] and asymptotic methods [3] more universally applicable and solvable in a computationally cheap way.
Another field preprocessing aspect, denoising, under similar assumptions as the field extension technique, has shown to be amenable to an effective treatment on synthetic examples. In reality, the measurement polution is more complicated and appropriate noise model has yet to be deduced and dealt with.
Finally, processing the field data obtained through QDM measurements rely on the methods developed for the SSM (Scanning SQUID Microscope) setup. While both settings invoke measurements of the magnetic field in only one direction (orthogonal to the measurement plane in case of SSM and along crystallographic axis in case of QDM), they are equivalent only under idealisation of fully available magnetic data, and hence in a realistic situation of partial measurements, there are clear benefits of the separate development of the QDM methodology analogous to that of SSM.
The PhD thesis will involve theoretical design, numerical implementation and analysis of the methods, testing on synthetic and experimental data and comparison with alternative approaches. It hence requires the candidate to have a solid background in applied/computational mathematics with strong interest in physical applications.
