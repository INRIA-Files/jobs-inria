Context

This PhD thesis will take place in the AIRSEA team, at Jean Kuntzmann Lab (LJK), Grenoble. AIRSEA is a research team in applied mathematics, which scientific activities are focused on the design of mathematical and numerical methods for numerical forecast systems of the ocean and the atmosphere. There will be also interactions with oceanographers and climate scientists in Brest, Marseille and Toulouse, in the context of the mediation project.

Assignment

In the current context of rapid climate change, numerical models are important tools for predicting climate change and assisting decision making by policy makers (e.g. in terms of protection of marine areas, land use or definition of fishing quotas). Two major challenges for the coming years are, on the one hand, to improve climate projections on a regional scale (what will be the local evolutions ?) and, on the other hand, to estimate the uncertainty on these regional projections.

Our team is involved in a major French project in numerical ocean modeling, selected within the Priority Research Program “An Ocean of Solutions”1. This five-year project, named mediation2, began at the end of 2022. It brings together several French scientific teams, ranging from applied mathematics and computer science to ocean physics and marine biology. One objective of this project is to build a chain of models ranging from the simulation of the physics (currents, temperature, salinity) to the trophic levels (plankton, fish). The study of the impact of climate change on the results requires the definition of scenarios for energy, land use and fisheries. Each scenario corresponds to a combination of parameters, which will influence the output of the numerical chain.

However, the huge complexity of the models and the generally very high cost of numerical simulations make an exhaustive exploration of the parameter space, corresponding to all possible scenarios and all model internal options, completely illusory. The idea is therefore to exploit statistical tools for the design of experiments. These tools make it possible to identify specific combinations that provide maximum information on a given quantity of interest (for example an indicator of ecosystem health) calculated from the simulation performed. The design of experiments also has the advantage that it can be built adaptively, to take into account pre-existing simulations. This is particularly appropriate for our appli- cation, where the number of possible simulations is very low. Moreover, in our specific context, the results of numerous simulations previously carried out are available, including in different settings, with different resolutions, different models, etc. From a statistical point of view, the theoretical tools for addressing these issues are sequential design of experiments, enrichment strategies, and multi-fidelity Gaussian process regression.

In this context, the main objectives of this PhD are:

  •  to make an inventory of the existing literature on this subject, and make a critical synthesis;
  • to collect the results of previous simulations, and study what indicators can be calculated from these results;
  • to formulate the problem of exploring political scenarios in the context of these adaptive experimental designs;
