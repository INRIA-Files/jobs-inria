2023-06132 - PhD Position F/M Mechanistic Modeling: application in vaccine development (IDP 2023)

Fonction : Doctorant

Contexte et atouts du poste

The mechanisms behind the response to vaccination have been described by immunology [1]. However, the quantitative modeling approach is made challenging by 1/ an imperfect knowledge of the exact biological mechanisms and 2/ uncertainties in the measurements of the biological processes in play.


Mathematical modeling of the response triggered by the vaccine consists of analyzing the dynamics of cell populations. On the one hand, it is unclear what type of cells should be considered in this modeling, and any modeling is always an oversimplification of reality. On the other hand, these cell populations are often measured by flow cytometry, which is a resource-intensive analysis. Gene expression as measured in whole blood can provide untargeted information on cell populations. For example, the Coverage Immuno project led by the SISTM team has shown that it is possible to study gene expression by sequencing over drops of blood collected by prick tests from patients themselves [2]. Analyzing the expression of 20,000 genes at each time point may allow us to track the abundance of cell populations as well as the dynamics of biological metabolic pathways [3]. In this project we will address the general question of which and how longitudinal transcriptomic markers describe well the process of establishment of an immune response after vaccination. Therefore, this project needs to bridge the gap between mechanistic modeling and high-dimensional data.

A latent variable model is a statistical model that relates a set of observable variables/markers (here transcriptomics, possibly preselected [4]) to a set of latent variables/processes (here cell populations). The central assumption is that each observed marker is a noisy measure of an underlying latent process of interest, and different markers may come from the same latent process. These models have been developed in the context of multivariate mixed longitudinal data to describe latent constructs such as cognition or quality of life or functional dependency [5]. In this past work, latent processes were assumed to follow a mixed effects model. We propose to extend these statistical methods by adding interpretability and mechanistic knowledge in the latent processes. To do this, we will rely on mechanistic models, which are non-linear mixed effects models based on ordinary differential equations [6]. This will help ensure the identifiability of the final model, which is particularly important in this high-dimensional context where the number of observed variables is really larger than the number of individuals observed longitudinally.

 

 

Mission confiée

The PhD thesis is jointly supervised by Cécile Proust-Lima (Inserm U1219, Biostat Team), an expert in latent process models, and Mélanie Prague (Inria Bordeaux Sud-ouest), an expert in mechanistic models.


“Use of latent models to build mechanistic models in high dimension: application in immunology for vaccine development”


The first part of the PhD will consist in solving the latent variable problem in mechanistic models described above, when the differential equation admits an analytical solution and when the relationship between markers and latent processes does not evolve over time. The PhD student will develop methods based on likelihood maximization to solve the estimation problem. Meanwhile, methods based on Lasso or Random Forests [7] will be integrated to statistically learn which markers contribute to the unobserved latent processes and account for the high-dimensional structure. Then, the problem will be extended to problems for which there is no analytical solution. We will investigate the usefulness of using existing software to solve nonlinear mixed-effects models, such as Monolix [8], and re-implement a SAEM-type algorithm for estimation, if necessary. Finally, we will explore the possibility of extending the methods for situations where time has an impact on the relationship between markers and latent processes, possibly using methods based on longitudinal random forests [9]. All methods will be published in peer-reviewed articles and the code will be distributed via Github and possibly an R package.

The application will focus on mechanistic models for B-cell responses to vaccination as published in Pasin et al [10], Balelli et al [11], and Clairon et al [12]. The team already has datasets from several projects, notably on Ebola vaccination (European IMI2 projects Ebovac 1/2/3; European EDTCP project PREVAC/PREVAC- UP) and SARS-CoV-2 vaccination (in collaboration with the Vaccine Research Institute). Thus, methods will be directly applied to data.

A propos d'Inria

Inria est l’institut national de recherche dédié aux sciences et technologies du numérique. Il emploie 2600 personnes. Ses 200 équipes-projets agiles, en général communes avec des partenaires académiques, impliquent plus de 3500 scientifiques pour relever les défis du numérique, souvent à l’interface d’autres disciplines. L’institut fait appel à de nombreux talents dans plus d’une quarantaine de métiers différents. 900 personnels d’appui à la recherche et à l’innovation contribuent à faire émerger et grandir des projets scientifiques ou entrepreneuriaux qui impactent le monde. Inria travaille avec de nombreuses entreprises et a accompagné la création de plus de 180 start-up. L'institut s'eﬀorce ainsi de répondre aux enjeux de la transformation numérique de la science, de la société et de l'économie.
