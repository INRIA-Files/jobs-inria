Assignment


Executive summary

This Ph.D. project aims at developing new numerical tools for the simulation of the motion of particles in the near-wall region. It draws on recent experimental measurements that provided direct evidence of the role of inter-particle collisions and clustering in dense monolayer deposits [4]. Using state-of-the-art fine-scale simulations of particle motion [5], large-scale models will be developed by reproducing both individual and collective motion. Such developments will pave the way towards unified models that can be applied to complex multilayer systems (e.g., with avalanches).

General context:

The physics of two-phase flows: Solid particles suspended in a flow (either gas or liquid) are omnipresent in our every-day life. For instance, in atmospheric sciences, pollens are dispersed by strong winds and can affect the air quality. Similarly, radioactive contamination following nuclear incidents can be spread by the wind over large distances. In marine systems, the water quality can severely deteriorate when plastic debris remobilize from riverbeds or when biological deposits resuspend from the interior of pipes [2].

These selected examples illustrate the rich complexity of the phenomena related to particle-laden flows. In fact, the fluid is chaotic due to turbulence while particles are polydisperse, of any shape and possibly deformable (like leaves). These particles can collide together in the fluid and form larger agglomerates. They have complex interactions with surfaces, on which they can deposit and later be resuspended. Addressing these phenomena thus requires to handle trans-disciplinary topics, involving issues related to: fluid mechanics, interface chemistry, surface and material science.

Numerical simulations: To address such challenges, researchers and engineers are increasingly relying on simulations using numerical tools. This has led to the development of various approaches, within the field of ``Computational Fluid Dynamics'' (CFD). Yet, developing models that remain applicable in large-scale situations requires to come up with reduced descriptions. This brings out the need for statistical descriptions that capture the complexity of these phenomena, which span a range of temporal and spatial scales (e.g., from adhesion over the nanoscale to transport over several meters//kilometers) and fields (e.g., physics, chemistry).  

State of art on modeling approaches

This Ph.D. project is focused on particle resuspension. Resuspension refers to the physical process by which a bed of solid microscopic particles adhering to a surface is re-entrained into a gas due to the action of the flow. Despite a century of research, the underlying physical mechanisms are only partially understood and, hence, numerical models are still incomplete [2, 3].

Existing models have been designed to treat two limiting cases: sparse monolayers or multilayer deposits. In sparse monolayers, particles are implicitly assumed to be far enough from each other such that each resuspension event can be treated independently. This has led to a number of accurate models for individual events [2]. In multilayer systems, particles interact with each other and the deposit morphology profoundly affects the number of moving particles. Owing to this complexity, existing models are simply based on crude statistics of the deposit structure (e.g., its porosity). Hence, unified descriptions of particle resuspension are still missing.

Another limitation is that most (if not all) of the models have been designed with the sole purpose of capturing the resuspension rate. This rate measures the average number of particles removed from a surface during a fixed amount of time. As such, this rate does provide information only on a mean-field quantity and no data is available on instantaneous quantities and their distribution in time/space. Hence, future models should contain more detailed information (especially regarding correlated resuspension events in turbulent flows).

 

Main activities

Objectives

This project aims at addressing the shortcomings identified before in terms of models. This includes three main objectives:

  • To develop new models that cover the whole range of situations from sparse monolayer to multilayer beds of particles, by identifying the key mechanisms and reproducing their effect on resuspension through statistical approaches;
  • To explore the role of correlated events in the instantaneous resuspension rate and to develop models that accurately reproduce these effects;
  • To develop new algorithm that are compatible with standard CFD approaches while including detailed statistical information (e.g., on correlations);

Methodology

To reach these objectives, the project draws on two recent data on particle resuspension. On the one hand, new experimental data on dense monolayer systems has revealed the role played by collision-propagation effects and clustering [4]. On the other hand, detailed information on the flow field around deposited particles and their near-wall dynamics has been recently obtained [5] with Particle Resolved Direct Numerical Simulations PR-DNS (which consists in solving explicitly the flow around finite-sized particles and their corresponding dynamics).

More precisely, the project aims at developing new stochastic Lagrangian models that remain tractable in large-scale simulations. For that purpose, new physical models will be developed drawing on the novel effects identified in the recent experiments and implemented in fine-scale simulations (relying on DNS). Second, sensitivity analysis techniques will be used directly on the results obtained from such microscopic simulations. This analysis will help design reduced statistical model that include information on correlated events and correlated particle dynamics.

Finally, the resulting Lagrangian stochastic model will be coupled to existing CFD software for two-phase flows, especially the approach available within the team. This will allow to validate the model using recent experimental data. If possible, the model can also be tested later in more complex multilayer cases (e.g., to evaluate its ability to reproduce avalanches).
