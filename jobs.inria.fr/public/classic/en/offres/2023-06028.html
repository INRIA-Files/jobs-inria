Context

The PhD project aims at developing a non-smooth modelling of granular flows down an incline disturbed by singularities in complex geometries in three dimensions. This modelling aims to investigate in detail the coupling between the mean hydrodynamic behaviour of the grains’ assembly in interaction with a structure on the one hand and the discrete nature of the material at stake and the resulting fluctuations on the other hand. An increased complexity will be implemented in the modelling approach not only in terms of the granular material type considered (dry non-cohesive or cohesive) but also in term of the obstacle which disturbs the flow (fixed and rigid structure versus deformable structure). The objective is to achieve a sufficiently realistic simulation of the interaction of shallow granular flows (with a few grains across the thickness) involved in nature, like dense snow avalanches or rockfalls, in interaction with singularities: topographic accidents, fixed and rigid obstacle, deformable structure, etc.

The main objective is to develop discrete numerical models, based on non-smooth contact dynamics, to simulate thin, sloping and interacting grain flows with singularities in realistic three-dimensional (3D) geometrical configurations, with a focus on understanding and quantifying the coupling between the hydrodynamics of the flow (thickness-averaged behavior) and the granular nature of the flowing material (fluctuations related in particular to the finite size of the grains and their relatively low number in the thickness of the flows). We will also be interested in the fluid/structure couplings and the resulting impact forces on the obstacles, especially in the case of deformable structures.

The proposed work will rely on the use of non-smooth mechanics [Acary & Brogliato, 2008], following the work presented in [Bourrier & Acary, 2022; Collins-Craft et al, 2022; Acary et al, 2023], to analyze the physical processes involved in the interaction of a grain flow with singularities. The main tasks of the research work are:

  • The development of 3D numerical simulations involving granular materials of increasing complexity (dry non-cohesive grains, cohesive grains) interacting with rigid and deformable structures,
  • The optimization of the numerical model to allow extensive numerical simulations for 3D geometry problems on a high performance computing architecture,
  • Conducting detailed physical analyses of the interaction not only for simplified configurations but also for complex geometric cases, with a focus on the coupling between the hydrodynamic patterns formed (juts, dead zones, jets, etc. [Faug, 2015]) and the discrete nature of the flowing material as well as on the effects of this coupling on the impact forces on the structures


The PhD student will benefit from the international network of the supervisors, in particular the very active collaboration of Thierry Faug (UMR IGE, ECRINS team) with the University of Sydney and the research group of Itai Einav at the School of Civil Engineering (Australia), on the problem of granular flows and the experimental measurement of these flows using dynamic X-ray radiography [Méjean et al, 2022]. The available and unique data sets obtained with the Australian team (3D density and velocity profile measurements in highly non-uniform granular flows) will be valuable for the calibration and validation of the numerical simulations developed by the PhD student. The more recent collaboration of the TRIPOP team at Inria with ETHZ and Johan Gaume's research group at WSL (Switzerland) will allow the PhD student to confront the discrete numerical modeling developed in the framework of the thesis with continuous approaches such as the MPM (Material Point Method).

Main activities

The PhD work is mainly based on the development of a numerical model based on the non-smooth dynamic simulation of a large number of grains (from several hundred thousand to one million) in three-dimensional geometric configurations. The PhD student will have access to all the numerical means available via the two laboratories involved: PCs and computing servers at the local level, via the computing resources of the research teams/units of the three supervisors and of the University of Grenoble Alpes, but also at the national level (via the supervising research organizations: INRAE and Inria).

Skills

The PhD candidate should have competences in mechanics and numerical modelling. A strong theoretical background in fluid and solid mechanics is mandatory. Furthermore, the applicant must show a strong interest for software development in computational mechanics. He also has to be motivated by applied research in collaboration with researchers from different disciplines. A good level of English and subsequent writing capacities are also requested.
