Assignment

Background

Since more than a decade, immune-checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) have led to drastic improvement of survival rates in the treatment of advanced stages of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)1. Despite these major advances with ~ 20% of long-term responders2, there is a wide variability in clinical response and progression eventually occurs in a large majority of patients.

There is a crucial unmet clinical need to identify biomarkers predictive of response 2.

To date, only a single baseline marker (PDL1 expression) is routinely used, with poor performances for prediction of durable response (AUC = 0.601) 3. We posit that multi-modal data could yield integrative models with improved predictive power. In addition, our recent results have demonstrated that the integration of longitudinal data substantially improves survival prediction (AUC = 0.857)4,5 . These findings were obtained with basic tumor size and data of 4 blood markers. Integration of more advanced pre-treatment data characterizing immune contexture (e.g., immune-monitoring or multiplex immonohistochemistry) has also been found to improve predictive abilities6,7. Thus, combining these two aspects (longitudinal + multi-modal) holds premise to further refine predictions. However, this brings novel computational challenges in order to integrate these specific data types into machine learning predictive models.

Objective

To develop machine learning (ML) methods able to deal with high-dimensional, multi-modal and longitudinal data containing missing values, in order to predict response to ICI in NSCLC.

Data

We have access to two unique and highly valuable clinical data sets:

    1) The PIONeeR-biomarkers RHU clinical study (“Precision Immuno-Oncology for advanced Non-small cell lung cancer patients with PD-(L)1 ICI Resistance”) (N = 450). Multi-modal and longitudinal data consist of:

            - at baseline and 6 weeks (two biopsies of tumor tissue, which is a unique feature of this data):

  1. i)  multiplex immunohistochemistry (IHC) (p = 122 variables)
  2. ii) Whole-exome and transcriptomic sequencing data (p50,000 x 2)

            iii) pathology (PDL1 expression and mutations) (p=28)

            - at baseline and 6 additional time points (blood sampling)

  1. iv) immune-monitoring from flow cytometry (p=276)
  2. v) vasculo-monitoring and soluble factors (p=15)
  3. vi) Individual lesions sizes

            vii) pharmacokinetics (p = 2)

            viii) clinical biomarkers from blood sampling (hematology, biochemistry, p = 41)

    2) The GFPC PERSEE phase III clinical trial data (N = 262):

  1. i) longitudinal imaging data (CT scans and positron emission tomography)
  2. ii) longitudinal clinical biomarkers from blood sampling (hematology, biochemistry)

Accessibility of clinical data is a crucial point.

For 1), COMPO is already in charge of the biostatistical and modeling analysis of the data. The current dataset contains data from 325 patients and the final 450 patients lock is expected for October 2023. Current NA (missing values) rate is 50% overall.

For 2), the trial is a national-level phase III trial sponsored by the French Group of Pulmonary Oncology (GFPC, head Pr Greillier, co-supervisor of the PhD). Its clinical aim is to evaluate pembrolizumab in monotherapy versus pembrolizumab in combination with platinum-based chemotherapy in patients with advanced non-small cell lung cancer and high PD-L1 expression (≥50% of malignant cells). GFPC PERSEE study is the only randomized trial in the world addressing this very important and pragmatic medical question in a Caucasian population. An agreement has already been established with the GFPC to secure data access.

Methodological challenges

Multiple computational methodological challenges emerge from the concrete clinical problem and data:

  • Missing values
  • Multi-modal data
  • Longitudinal data
  • High-dimensional data (imaging and omics)
  • Survival (censored) data

Methodology

Two methodological avenues of research will be investigated that are relevant to addressing the objective using the data: 1) missing values, 2) longitudinal data.

For the first point, research about management of missing values and their imputation is an active field of machine learning research8. The candidate will depart from state-of-the art methods to develop novel algorithms adapted to the specificity of the clinical problem and data. To do so, we envision to first investigate the coupling of imputation methods with recent survival models taking advantage of deep learning techniques. 

For the second point, first standard ML methods (Logistic regression, Random Forest, SVMs, etc.) will be used on pre-treatment radiomics features in order to predict treatment response and toxicity. Since the amount of data is relatively low (a maximum of 260 patients), these methods should offer competitive results compared with deep learning approaches. Then, the longitudinal aspects will be integrated using delta-radiomics, i.e. deriving simple longitudinal metrics (e.g., ratios) from radiomics features at multiple times. Then our focus will be to study several types of deep learning models that can cope with low amount of data, transfer capabilities and domain adaptation from related larger datasets. Moreover, the proposed methods should produce a compact latent representation that contains the information required for the recognition to allow further comparison with radiomics. Related available datasets will be collected to increase the amount of data to learn from. Then various transfer or adaptation methods can be studied and evaluated as simple finetuning 11. Augmentation on the available data will also be investigated while respecting the constraints of the medical imaging systems. Then, we will develop compact latent representations using unsupervised methods such as Auto-Encoders and Variational Auto-Encoders. These methods will be compared with self-supervised methods such as clustering methods or contrastive based methods. These recent methods showed great capabilities to learn discriminative representations. Eventually, combination of the longitudinal imaging + biological data will need to be addressed in order to apply the methods to the datasets. Candidate models will be benchmarked based on their predictive abilities.
