Context

Advised by : Marie Babel and Vincent Drevelle

Mobility aids such as power wheelchairs can compensate for the loss of mobility experienced by elderly or disabled people in order to maintain a level of independence and access to social activities [1]. However, driving them is a difficult task requiring cognitive and visuospatial abilities, especially when negotiating obstacles and narrow spaces [2].

Many works are focused on robotic wheelchairs, capable of autonomous navigation [3]. However, autonomous navigation solutions pose problems of acceptability and do not give the user sufficient control, which is necessary to solicit the remaining cognitive and motor functions [4].

Semi-autonomous approaches, based on shared control or guidance [5], are thus better adapted to the user's needs, leaving him/her sufficient control. The Rainbow team of IRISA/Inria has designed a driving assistance solution based on sensor-based servoing. It reactively corrects the trajectory when approaching positive [6] or negative [7] obstacles and allows the improvement of driving performances.

However, this proposal is not sufficient to ensure medium- or long-term navigation tasks, alternating shared and autonomous control. Consider the following life scenario, where a resident wishes to return to bed. While retaining control of the wheelchair, he could be guided through the facility to his room and then assisted with precise positioning of the wheelchair near the bed to complete his transfer safely. Once the transfer is done, the empty chair should be able to automatically reposition itself in the room to be recharged. Then, on demand, the wheelchair can be recalled to the transfer position near the bed.

Assignment

New sensors are emerging to achieve this goal of semi-autonomous navigation, while respecting the constraints of low cost, integration on the wheelchair and respect of privacy.

  • Time-of-flight (ToF) infrared sensors, currently used for obstacle avoidance, can be used for precise positioning near an object.
  • Ultra wide band (UWB) radio systems allow the measurement of distances between fixed beacons and a mobile sensor, and provide a localization solution for navigation.

The objective of this thesis is to develop sensor-based control methods, autonomous or shared, taking fully into account the parametric uncertainties and measurement errors of the UWB and ToF sensors implemented on the wheelchair.

Different issues need to be addressed:

  • UWB sensor measurements are affected by outliers: masking of the direct line of sight by the user, the wheelchair or the environment, multipath and reception of reflected signals (NLoS).
  • The electric wheelchair is subject to a non-holonomic movement constraint. Its spatial footprint and our goal of achieving a user-defined position and orientation configuration mean that it cannot be reduced to a simple point whose movement we would control.
  • The evolution of the wheelchair cannot be modeled exactly: the motor control is disturbed by weight variations and slope, the wheels can slip or skid, and the presence of idle wheels complicates the real movement of the wheelchair.

For this thesis, we will use the set-membership approach, assuming bounded errors. The parameters and measurements will be modeled by intervals assumed to contain the true value. The tools of interval analysis [8] such as set-inversion, guaranteed integration [9] and constraint propagation on tubes [10] will be used to propagate these uncertainties in a rigorous way and characterize solutions in the form of sets [11]. Outliers can be handled by robust assembly approaches such as q-relaxed intersection.

Main activities

We will seek to plan and guarantee the feasibility of precise approach and positioning maneuvers (such as positioning next to the bed, or docking at a charging station) taking into account parametric and measurement uncertainties, non-holonomic movement constraints of the wheelchair, and guaranteeing the absence of collisions with the environment.

In the context of the shared control of the wheelchair, predictive methods will be studied in order to determine the space of admissible user commands, according to the constraints related to safety (collisions, falls) and driving comfort, and the associated uncertainties.

The developed methods will be developed on the team's wheelchair simulator. They will be experimentally validated on Rainbow's fleet of robotic wheelchairs, using a motion capture system as a position reference. As part of the shared control, the PhD student will carry out clinical studies, with the help of clinicians from the Pôle Saint-Hélier rehabilitation center.
