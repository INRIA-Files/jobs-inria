2023-05735 - PhD Position F/M Designing Multimodal Human-Computer Partnership for Music Practice

Contract type : Fixed-term contract

Level of qualifications required : Graduate degree or equivalent

Fonction : PhD Position

Context

The ExSitu research group is exploring how to create true human-computer partnerships, taking advantage of computational abilities, but leaving the user in control. We build on the principles of instrumental interaction [1, 2] and co-adaptation [6] to create interactive systems that are discoverable [7], appropriable [3, 5], and expressive [4], that grow with the user to enhance rather than replace the user’s skills.

Professional musicians are a particularly demanding audience, who seek to generate new compositions or forms of musical expression. However, today’s professional music tools focus primarily on implementing existing ideas, such as rendering notes onto a scale, rather them helping musicians as they generate new ideas. ExSitu’s research focus is on developing interactive tools and systems that support fluid interaction between creative professionals and their tools. We seek to create expressive human-computer partnerships, where the tools help users explore ideas according to their expertise, experience and personal taste, while adapting to changing goals and contexts.

This Ph.D. focuses on music professionals, both composers and musicians. The goal is to explore how different modalities, such as gesture and gaze, can help create a more effective human-computer partnership allows the user to remain in control without disturbing their creative flow.

The candidate will be part of the ExSitu research team, based at Inria Paris-Saclay in France. The 3-year doctoral position is fully funded and will be supervised by Prof. Wendy Mackay and Dr. Janin Koch.

Assignment

The goal of this Ph.D. is to design and develop a novel form of human-computer partnership in the context of professional music practice. The student will build on ExSitu’s theory-based interaction approaches to develop systems that help users to interactively explore diverse capabilities of music systems, with alternative modalities, such as gesture or gaze.

The student will develop new design and evaluation methods that support effective interaction, specifically:

  • Provideabetterunderstandingofhowusersdirectlyandindirectlycommunicatewhileplayingmusictogether.

  • Identify the relevant characteristics of such interaction, including breakdowns and workarounds.

  • Design new interactive methods for users to express and refine these characteristics related to their particular

    music or instrument style.

  • Develop working prototype(s) that demonstrate these methods in realistic music performances.

  • Develop and apply evaluation methods to determine the efficacy of the interaction from a human perspective.

Main activities

Specific Activities

The doctoral candidate will be expected to:

  • Conduct empirical studies and workshops, e.g. participatory design workshops.
  • Prototype, design and develop novel interactive systems.
  • Design, run, and analyze qualitative and quantitative studies to evaluate interaction techniques.
  • Write and publish research articles for top-level research venues.

 

Expected Results

  • Advance our understanding about how musicians can use different modalities to steer and control a human- computer partnership.
  • Novel interaction methods for exploring musical ideas.
  • Working interactive system(s) that demonstrate new interactive approaches in music practice.

Skills

We are looking for motivated students who are excited about music and designing practice-focused exploratory tools. Suitable candidates should have experience in Human-Computer Interaction methods and programming skills, preferably Python, are required.

Background knowledge in some music practice and experience in web technologies is a plus.

The doctoral position will be in English.
