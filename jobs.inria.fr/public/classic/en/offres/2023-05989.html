2023-05989 - PhD Position F/M [Campagne CORDI-S] Abstract Interpretation-Based Static Analyses for Robust (Un)Reachability

Contract type : Fixed-term contract

Level of qualifications required : Graduate degree or equivalent

Fonction : PhD Position

Context

The advent of big data — the manipulation and analysis of massive quantities of data – has revolutionized the world of software development in the past decade. Every day, data scientists develop short computer programs to clean, analyze, process, and visualize data. At the same time, we are witnessing widespread adoption of such data-manipulating programs for aiding or even automating decisions with far-reaching societal impact, e.g., in field such as social welfare, criminal justice, or even healthcare. As we rely more and more on data-manipulating decision-making programs, we become increasingly vulnerable to logical, programming or technical mistakes. In particular, mistakes that do not cause software failures can have serious consequences, since they give no indication that something went wrong. Just to name a recent case, a simple technical mistake made during data processing caused nearly 16,000 cases of Covid-19 between September 25th and October 2nd, 2020 to go unreported from official figures in the UK. As a consequence, Public Health England was unable to send out the relevant contact-tracing alerts (https://www.bmj.com/content/bmj/371/bmj.m3891.full.pdf). Mistakes in medical applications can be deadly.

In the context of traditional (non data science-related) software systems, formal methods have been used in order to provide strong, mathematically-grounded proofs of correctness. They are used at an industrial level in avionics, which has very stringent verification requirements mandated by international standards (DO-178C). This success is due to the recognition of formal methods by standards and the availability of effective, efficient verification tools. Among formal verification techniques, static analyzers based on abstract interpretation live in a sweet spot as they are fully automated, efficient, and sound by construction. A notable example is the the Astrée analyzer used to ensure the absence of run-time errors in critical avionics C code. By contrast, the use of formal verification techniques in data science is still extremely limited.

The goal of the PhD is to explore the use of static analysis by abstract interpretation to verify data-manipulating software, with a particular focus on data science notebooks (such as Jupyter notebooks). The ANTIQUE project team at Inria is expert in verification by abstract interpretation and application to avionics and has a growing expertise in the analysis of data science and machine-learned software.

Assignment

The vast majority of the state of the art in formal methods focuses on the verification of safety properties, which ultimately amount to verifying the (un)reachability of some bad or error state. Recently, the notion of robust reachability has been proposed as a refinement of standard reachability: a bad or error state is robustly reachable if there exists values for the controlled inputs such that the bad or error state is reached independently of the values of the uncontrolled inputs. Robust reachability is better suited than standard reachability in many realistic situations related to security or software engineering.

Our aim is to study the verification of robust reachability properties for data-manipulating software, where reasoning about how inputs (i.e., data) are used and detecting those with disproportionate impact on the result of some computation (independently of the values of the other input data) is crucial to detect possibly serious mistakes. For instance, such properties would be useful for reasoning about fairness, i.e., where non-sensitive data should determine the result of the computation independently of the value of the sensitive data.

Equally interesting is the dual property of robust unreachability, i.e., a state is robustly unreachable if for all values of controlled inputs there exists values for the uncontrolled inputs such that the state is unreachable. We also intend to consider quantitative variants of these properties for more nuanced and informative verification results, i.e., quantifying the impact of some input data on the result of a computation.

Main activities

Robust (un)reachability can be formally expressed using Computation Tree Logic (CTL). We plan to revisit and build upon an existing static analysis framework proposed for termination, liveness properties, and general CTL properties to design a static analysis-based approach for (quantitative) robust (un)reachability.

In particular, the existing framework will need to be drastically evolved to be able to reason about multidimensional data typically manipulated by data science notebooks. This will require defining new abstract domains, the core of all abstract interpreters. Both semantic aspects (expressiveness, abstract algebra) and algorithmic aspects (data-structures, algorithms, complexity) must be considered to allow a theoretical analysis and an effective implementation. We expect to build upon and adapt existing general purpose (numerical, string, etc.) abstract domains and combine these with data structure and domain-specific abstractions. Because abstract domains embed a precision vs. performance tradeoff, experimental proof is paramount to justify the ability of the method to provide meaningful results in reasonable times on real-world data science notebooks (e.g., available on GitHub or Kaggle). Extensive experimental evaluations will provide feedback to hone abstract domains and achieve scalability as larger and more complex notebooks are considered.

Finally, we also plan to adapt our static analysis to the dynamic read-eval-print-loop (REPL) experience that data science notebooks provide. This will require rendering the analysis sufficiently lightweight and fast to be interactive, to run along the development and execution of a notebook, and able to backtrack when necessary, to adapt to the out-of-order execution model of notebook code cells.

About Inria

Inria is the French national research institute dedicated to digital science and technology. It employs 2,600 people. Its 200 agile project teams, generally run jointly with academic partners, include more than 3,500 scientists and engineers working to meet the challenges of digital technology, often at the interface with other disciplines. The Institute also employs numerous talents in over forty different professions. 900 research support staff contribute to the preparation and development of scientific and entrepreneurial projects that have a worldwide impact.
