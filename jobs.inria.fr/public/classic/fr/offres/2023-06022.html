Contexte et atouts du poste

General Description

The goal of this PhD is to design a disruptive distributed spatial audio system compatible with most virtual sound field synthesis techniques (e.g., Ambisonics, Wave Field Synthesis, etc.). This should allow us to significantly reduce the cost and improve the accessibility of spatial audio systems which are increasingly used nowadays for a wide range of applications: virtual reality, movie theaters, concert arenas, etc.

Context

The successful candidate will join the Emeraude INRIA/INSA-Lyon research team which is based at the CITI Lab at INSA-Lyon (Villeurbanne, France). This PhD will be conducted under the supervision of Romain Michon and Tanguy Risset in close collaboration with the Multisensory Experience Lab at Aalborg University in Copenhagen (https://melcph.create.aau.dk/) with the help of Prof. Stefania Serafin. Regular trips will be planned to Copenhagen as part of this PhD work.

Mission confiée

Full PhD Topic

The field of spatial audio has experienced significant growth in recent years due to the increasing demand for immersive experiences in virtual reality, movie theaters, concert arenas, and other contexts. Similarly, auralization, which involves creating virtual acoustical spaces, is slowly becoming more prevalent in museums, concert halls, etc., and makes an extensive use of speaker arrays.

Spatial audio is based on a wide range of techniques (e.g., Ambisonics [1], Wave Field Synthesis [2], etc.) allowing us to create virtual sound fields, move sound in space, etc. All of these techniques share a common rule: the more speakers, the better (a little bit like pixels in an image). The main limitation to having more speakers in spatial audio systems is throughput: more speakers implies more computation and also sending more digital audio streams in parallel to a digital audio interface. Using standard equipment, designing spatial audio systems requiring more than 32 speakers is usually a complex task. Similarly, exceeding 64-speakers is almost impossible without using extremely expensive proprietary dedicated hardware. Indeed, companies such as l-acoustics offer solutions like l-isa (https://l-isa.l-acoustics.com/) to implement systems involving more than 64 speakers, but their cost makes it impossible to deploy them in most settings.

One potential solution to this 64 channel limit is to distribute computation. Curiously enough, this approach has not been studied much in the past [3-4]. The goal of this PhD is to explore the potential of using a network of small computing platforms to implement spatial audio systems. This presents multiple challenges:
- Each system in the network needs to be perfectly synchronized (a one sample offset is enough to "break" the perceptual effect of most spatial audio techniques).
- All clocks in the system must be perfectly synchronous.
- Distributing the computation of DSP algorithms implies a wide range of challenges too.
- Making a system that's easily programmable and that can "scale" is potentially complex in this context.

For this to work, "bare-metal" embedded systems must be used. Microcontroller platforms are a good candidate and these have increasingly been employed for real-time audio DSP in recent years [5]. For instance, systems such as the Teensy 4.1 (https://www.pjrc.com/store/teensy41.html) provide plenty of considerable computational power, Ethernet support, and good quality stereo audio output for less than fifty euros.

There is currently no open network protocol for streaming audio to this kind of distributed platform with perfect synchronicity. Hence, one of the first tasks of the successful candidate will be to design such a protocol. This work will build upon research initiated within the Emeraude team [6]. The next phase will consist in investigating the parallelization of various spatial audio techniques, implementing those techniques, and comparing the performance of the devised system with existing platforms. The Faust programming language (https://faust.grame.fr), which is also developed within the Emeraude team [7], will play a central role in making the system accessible and scalable.

We believe that this work has the potential to be highly disruptive in our field. The number of spatial audio systems in the world has exploded in recent years, especially in academia, and particularly due to increased research interest in virtual and augmented reality. That is why we are seeking an extremely motivated candidate with expertise in audio DSP, network audio, spatial audio, and the Faust programming language.

L'essentiel pour réussir

We're looking for a candidate with a background in audio digital signal processing and especially spatial audio techniques (i.e., ambisonics, WFS, etc.) along with a deep understanding of low-level digital audio systems. They should master C++ programming, especially in the context of audio, and have a good general knowledge about the Faust programming language. Some background on network protocols, especially that used in the context of audio (i.e., Dante, AVB, AES67, etc.) is highly appreciated too.

 
The ideal candidate should be self-driven and able to work independently as well as in collaboration with the rest of the Emeraude team.
