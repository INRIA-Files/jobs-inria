Contexte et atouts du poste

TRIPOP is a joint research team of Inria Grenoble Rhône-Alpes and of the Laboratoire Jean Kuntzmann and started in January 2018. The team is mainly concerned with the modeling, the mathematical analysis, the simulation and the control of nonsmooth dynamical systems, with a strong application to modeling natural environmental risks in mountains. Nonsmooth dynamics concerns the study of the time evolution of systems that are not smooth in the mathematical sense, i.e., systems that are characterized by a lack of differentiability, either of the mappings in theirs formulations, or of theirs solutions with respect to time. In mechanics, the main instances of nonsmooth dynamical systems are multibody systems with Signorini’s unilateral contact, set-valued (Coulomb-like) friction and impacts, or in continuum mechanics, ideal plasticity, fracture or damage. The members of the team have a long experience of nonsmooth dynamics modeling together with the development of simulation software.

Mission confiée

Keywords: data-driven modeling, Learning, Computational Mechanics, Contact and Impact Mechanics, Natural Hazard mitigation, cohesive and frictional materials.

In a context of climate change, the frequency of occurrence of gravity–driven hazards in mountains such as avalanches, mud flows, debris flows and rock flows is increasing. The prediction and mitigation of gravity risks related to it is an important challenge and the mathematical modeling and the numerical simulation of dynamical and mechanical processes are important tools to improve the knowledge on this phenomena and to be able to forecast it.

After years of constant progress in numerical methods and scientific computing, the main difficulty today in continuum mechanics lies in the formulation of behavioural equations for complex materials such as geomaterials (rocks, soils, clay and gravel, sand, snow, ice), or fluids with complex rheology (debris flows, mud). These materials are ubiquitous in applications related to natural hazards caused by mass movements under gravity. Thanks to data-based techniques, respecting the thermodynamics of continuous media, a new way is opening up to be able to describe behavioural laws in a robust and faithful manner to observations, and to integrate perfectly into the numerical modelling that is now classic in scientific computing (FEM, PFEM, MPM, FV, etc.).

Principales activités

The objectives of this PhD is to explore data-driven modeling techniques in a nonsmooth setting for discovering and modeling complex constitutive equations of geomaterials. The following items will be discussed and developed:

• Rethinking complex rheologies in the non-smooth framework. This will allow a simple and efficient modelling of threshold and inequality phenomena (one-sided contact, impact with Coulomb friction, threshold behaviour laws such as plasticity, damage or fracture, viscoplastic fluids of the Bingham and Herschel-Bulkley type) in order to develop new, implicit and robust numerical methods, where the most important physical features of frictional cohesive materials (geomaterials) are well modelled while neglecting second order phenomena.

• Use ”data-driven mechanics” approaches in the nonsmooth framework in cases where behavioural models are not reliable and faithful to the observed physical phenomena. These techniques make it possible to replace the laws of behaviour of materials with experimental data or data from smaller-scale simulations in order to model ”sub-mesh” phenomena, which are not or only partially taken into account in large-scale phenomenological models. Several techniques exist now. We refer, especially to [1] for data-driven mechanics, [2] for PINN, [3,4,5,6] for TANN and Bayesian approaches.

• Discover the governing equations of nonsmooth systems from trajectories given of basis of multi-valued functions as in the SINDy approch seems alsovery promising [7,8,9].
