About the research centre or Inria department

The research activities of members of Makutu share a common theme of using numerically computed wavefield measurements to reconstruct the propagation medium they passed through before recording. The medium can be reconstructed by identifying either the physical parameters or the geometrical parameters that characterize it. In each case, the next step is to solve an inverse problem that is non-linear and ill-posed. To solve it, Makutu is focusing on the Full Waveform Inversion (FWI), which is a high-definition imaging method widely used in the field of geophysics.

Context

In most scenarios studied by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS) play a very significant role in reaching long-term carbon neutrality goals. Realistic scenarios rely on carbon sequestration for as much as 10% of total CO2 emission reductions by 2050 [1]. As a consequence, the need for geological storage of CO2 is expected to grow very rapidly in the next few decades, from 30Mt/yr in 2018 to more than 0.7Gt/yr in 2030 and more than 2Gt/yr in 2050. Meeting this ambitious target in a safe, timely, and scalable manner requires numerical tools for seismic monitoring of CO2 injection operations, capable of tackling very large-scale problems, both in time and space.

Current technologies for the monitoring of CO2 injection operations rely on geophysical techniques such as seismic surveying, gravimetry, and electromagnetic methods. These approaches face new challenges today due to the different conditions imposed by CO2 injection operations. In particular, the need for new numerical approaches to simulate CO2 reservoirs and their monitoring operations is becoming more and more evident. One such challenge is posed by the problem of repeatability of acquisitions,
which requires treating accurately systematic errors arising from repeated measurements done at different points in time during the injection operations (also called 4D campaigns). Current techniques developed in the context of hydrocarbon exploration (see, e.g., [2]) will need adjustments in order to be ready to tackle the specificities of CO2 injection operations, including lower data availability, multiphysics integration, and different signal-to-noise ratio driven by different fluid effects.

Monitoring of CO2 operations also needs to rely on significantly cheaper and quicker acquisition methods compared to traditional exploration campaigns. Available datasets are inevitably more sparse, making it harder to rely on traditional, data-intensive imaging techniques such as Full Waveform Inversion (FWI) [1] to monitor safety-critical information about the shape and distribution of the CO2 plume or the overpressures near faults. Additionally, new acquisition techniques such as Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS) require new, inventive ways of using the available data for the best result. Finally, an accrued understanding of CO2 physics and its interaction is made difficult by the limited access to large-scale field data, which is still lacking. Having a clearer model of CO2 plume distributions and CO2-water interaction would be very useful for geostatistical purposes (such as multi-realization analysis) as well as to reduce the space of parameters used in the inversion. This is particularly useful since the number of inversion degrees of freedom is known to correlate very negatively (exponentially) with the conditioning of the inverse problem [2]. Tackling all these issues will certainly represent a challenge for current numerical schemes, algorithms, and simulators. Thankfully, some recent Machine Learning (ML) techniques seem to be well positioned to give a significant contribution to these problems, complementing traditional approaches rooted in numerical analysis.

[1] B. L. Biondi. 3D seismic imaging. Society of Exploration Geophysicists, 2006.
[2] E. Beretta, M. V. De Hoop, F. Faucher, and O. Scherzer. “Inverse boundary value problem for the Helmholtz equation: quantitative conditional Lipschitz stability estimates”. In: SIAM Journal on Mathematical Analysis 48.6 (2016), pp. 3962–3983.

 

Assignment

The goal of this thesis is to investigate how current and future Machine Learning (ML) techniques can be used to improve CO2 injection monitoring techniques and help current approaches, based mainly on numerical analysis, raise the standards required for large-scale CO2 injection operations. In particular, classical noise-reduction neural networks used in image processing are employed more and more for physical systems (see e.g. [5]), and represent good candidate solutions to acquisition repeatability issues. Using current simulation codes, one can generate synthetic data that includes sources of systematic errors such as source and receiver position, reservoir uplifting, and specific reservoir response function change, which can be used to train networks and models. One of the goals of this thesis is therefore to investigate how these techniques can be adapted to include the knowledge of the spatial distribution of the acquisition geometry, and used to distinguish the specific signature of systematic error in repeated seismic acquisition campaigns.


Recent works on supervised learning for geophysics applications [6] have shown that models based on convolutional neural networks (CNN) are able to achieve greater precision in the reconstruction of reservoir CO2 saturation distribution from surface gravimetry data, compared to standard inversion techniques. Adapting these approaches to seismic data, which is able to carry more information due to the hyperbolic character of its governing equations is a very promising research avenue and could lead to much-improved reservoir imaging with much sparser acquisition data. In particular, a significant reduction in the number of source shots can be achieved, with a positive effect on both the computational and the experimental cost of an acquisition campaign. One goal of this thesis is to investigate the role of similar supervised learning models for direct reservoir inversion from seismic data. Furthermore, achieving these results paves the way for the integration of multiple physics (including, for example, gravity and electromagnetic waves, as well as reservoir production and injection data) into the constraining data of the reservoir model, leading to a much more complete and robust inversion scheme, which can be investigated during the course of the thesis. Finally, recent work on Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)-generated models for History Matching (see, e.g., [7]) show how these kinds of generators are able to create valuable datasets and reduced-order spaces for geological and geophysical inversion. One of the goals of this thesis is to investigate the usefulness of these techniques in the context of CO2 injection, where these networks can be used to create reduced spaces to improve the conditioning of classical inversion techniques (such as FWI), as well as for ML-based inversion. A comparison of the convergence rate and results of GANs with alternatives such as diffusion models (see, e.g., [8]) is also a very interesting possibility to be explored in the course of the thesis and would be an important (and new, as far as we know) contribution to the use of ML-based generative models in geophysical applications.
